By Jeff Hargarten • Star Tribune

Republicans have held a steady grip on congressional and state-level power for the past decade, and since 2016 have occupied the White House.

But despite years of this lopsided balance of power, several early signs point to the possibility of a “blue wave” that could sweep Democrats back into power across the country this November.

Could such a phenomenon wash over Minnesota this election cycle?

Examining previous midterm backlashes, along with demographic and polling trends, suggests that the answer could be yes. And if those indicators hold out, the DFL might have many reasons to celebrate on Election Night.

Democrats lead the generic ballot

On a national level, FiveThirtyEight's aggregated estimate of generic congressional ballot results give Democrats an edge over Republicans among likely voters headed into the November midterm.

As of Oct. 16, 2018, registered and likely voters preferred Democrats over Republicans by about 8 points.

Source: FiveThirtyEight | Graphic by Jeff Hargarten, Star Tribune | Download Data

Historically, the generic ballot has been fairly predictive of outcomes in midterm elections, and Democrats at this time have an edge similar to the one Republicans had in their 2010 wave.

The margins have narrowed over the months though, so time will tell if Democrats can maintain their momentum in these kinds of polls.

Polls point to strong up-ballot Democratic tickets

Democratic candidates lead Republicans and those who are undecided or voting third party in Minnesota's biggest statewide races, according to a Star Tribune Minnesota Poll from September 2018.

Source: Star Tribune Minnesota Poll | Graphic by Jeff Hargarten, Star Tribune

Additonally, a recent NBC News/Marist poll of likely voters showed similar trends with Sen. Amy Klobuchar leading (D) leading Jim Newburger (R) for the U.S. Senate race, Sen. Tina Smith (D) leading Karin Housley (R) in U.S. Senate special election and Tim Walz (D) leading Jeff Johnson (R) in the gubernatorial contest.

Aside from providing momentum for DFL candidates on top of the ballot, winning big statewide races tends to help the party's candidates further down the ballot, creating a coattails effect.

Democrats lead the "enthusiasm gap"

Since their defeat in 2016, Democrats have been gradually regaining ground in off-year and special elections across the country, including major upset victories in Republican areas once thought to be secure. This has signaled an increase in Democratic energy and voter engagement.

While Minnesota hasn't seen a special election the last couple years, past midterm election results reveal some ways to measure voter enthusiasm.

Most Minnesota congressional districts seem some kind of bump for the opposition party during midterm elections, which is often accompanied by a boost in voter turnout, something FiveThirtyEight refers to as the "enthusiasm gap."

Precincts have shifted in favor of the minority party in the past four midterm elections, signaling greater voter enthusiasm across the state.

Whether Democrats can sustain this momentum and enthusiasm remains to be seen, as well as whether Republicans can match or surpass that kind of energy to halt the effects of a midterm backlash.

Low presidential popularity

While President Trump isn't on this year's ballot, in many ways midterm elections are referudums on presidential job performance.

Because of this, the president's party usually loses seats on state and federal levels, and how many seems partially tied to approval ratings. Presidents with an approval rating under 50 percent usually suffer the greatest midterm losses.
